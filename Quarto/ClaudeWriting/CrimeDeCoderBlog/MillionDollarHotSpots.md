# Million Dollar HotSpots

Crime is not spatially random, but clusters into small areas with a high prevalence -- hot spots of crime. Targeting those areas with a [hot spots policing approach](/services/PredictiveAnalytics), such as increasing patrols or working with community members, is a common-sense application once your police department has identified those hot spots.

**CRIME** De-Coder has developed [a methodology to identify high intensity hot spots of crime](https://andrewpwheeler.com/2020/10/08/recent-papers-on-hot-spots-of-crime-in-dallas/) that have a high return-on-investment for police departments to conduct a hot spots intervention. It costs the police departments time and money to respond and investigate crimes. If you can prevent those crimes before they occur, it saves the department time and money.

[Peter Moskos had a recent blog post](https://qualitypolicing.com/shooting-victims-on-a-map/) identifying areas of shooting hot spots in Baltimore, and suggested it is worth-it to have a a series of officers assigned just to those areas. This prompted **CRIME** De-Coder to illustrate the methodology based on empirical data. In Baltimore, there is one location that **CRIME** De-Coder calls a Million-dollar-hotspot in Baltimore:

Note that this methodology explicitly under-estimates costs of crime. If you want to also estimate victim costs, [such as medical expenses](https://nypost.com/2023/01/07/nyc-shootings-cost-taxpayers-at-least-350-million-in-hospital-bills/), it will increase the cost of crime estimates by a large margin.

But as is, these million dollar hotspots are no-brainers for police departments to implement a hotspots strategy at. A hotspots strategy will likely save operational costs at these hotspots in the long run, not to mention additional benefits to the greater community in reduced number of policing incidents.
