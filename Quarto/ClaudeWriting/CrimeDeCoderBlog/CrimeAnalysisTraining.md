## Crime Analysis Training

Crime analysis is often a mix of intelligence analysis and numerical data analysis. The main mission of **CRIME** De-Coder is to advance the use of numerical analysis in helping police (and other public and private sector agencies) be more efficient and effective in their organizations. Training individuals with real world examples is a critical part to accomplish that goal.

Over the **CRIME** De-Coder's career, I have developed and delivered training for crime analysts in various capacities. These include:

 - short in-person courses tailored to your specific agency over the course of a week
 - single workshops (in person or via web-recording) to discuss specific topics (have done workshops at [IACA](https://www.iaca.net/) and [ASEBP](https://americansebp.org/) in the past)
 - longer University style classes, such as [GIS](https://apwheele.github.io/Class_CrimeMapping/Lab00_ReadMe.html) and [Intro Crime Analysis using Microsoft Tools](https://apwheele.github.io/Class_CrimeAnalysis/Lab00_ReadMe.html). (I have previously taught at SUNY Albany and the University of Texas at Dallas both in-person and remote)

If you are interested in me developing custom courses for your agency, your organization, or your university, do not [hesitate to contact me](mailto:crimede-coder@crimede-coder.com).

### Tools for Crime Analysts

I have developed over my career various tools to make conducting more advanced statistical and geographic data analysis for crime analysts easier. These include:

 - Creating a statistical test, the Weighted Displacement Difference (Wheeler & Ratcliffe, 2018) to determine the amount of crime that has decreased in a hot spots intervention by simply inputting pre/post crime counts.
 - Creating a statistical test to identify if a serial spree of crimes tends to be committed during random days of the week (). (Also it can be applied to forensic accounting [Benford's analysis](https://andrewpwheeler.com/2017/03/31/using-the-exact-reference-distribution-for-small-sample-benford-tests/) with small samples.)
 - Implementing code to identify if low crime counts are different over time, the [Poisson e-test](https://andrewpwheeler.com/2018/04/29/testing-changes-in-short-run-crime-patterns-the-poisson-e-test/) or the Poisson z-score (Wheeler, 2016).
 - Developing standard temporal dashboards to identify crime shifts with low count crime data (wheeler, 2016). I have delivered this as training on multiple occasions, see my [IACA training materials](https://www.dropbox.com/s/ngq64tu7tv3hzdq/Wheeler_MonitorTemp_IACA_FinalFiles.zip?dl=0).

While much of my work focuses on more advanced statistical programming, I realize the majority of analysts use simpler tools, like Excel. This is especially critical for smaller agencies, who do not have the capacity to hire individuals with more advanced degrees.

If you are either interested in training, or me developing simpler spreadsheet or tools for your agency to use for various analysis tasks, do not hesitate [to contact the **CRIME** De-Coder](mailto:crimede-coder@crimede-coder.com) to see if I can help your agency.
