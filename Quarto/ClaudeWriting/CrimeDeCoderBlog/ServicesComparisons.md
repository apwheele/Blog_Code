It is really only 40 pages of substantive content.

So there are a few distinguishing factors between **CRIME** De-Coders service and this large consulting firm. Price and time are obvious ones (any analysis I would do would likely be done in less than 3 months, not several years). But a bigger factor is I don't just swoop in, drop a 400 page PDF, and go away. I would sit down and discuss your agencies needs and right size the scope of the project. Do you want to incorporate expected increases in calls for service in your growing city? Are you concerned about [recent recruitment trends and how it may impact response times](https://www.policechiefmagazine.org/linking-the-workforce-crisis-crime-and-response-time/)? Do you want to see how expanding your detective capacity may impact clearance rates?

So the plan is not only tailored for your particular circumstances, **CRIME** De-Coder is also cognizant of how public sector budgets are limited and can give advice to reduce costs of the services I provide. For a recent proposal the **CRIME** De-Coder had, I discussed two plans:

 - Plan A: $30,000 and **CRIME** De-Coder does 100% of the work.

So instead of charging your city to digitize different paper reports (what the consulting firm did for Dallas PD), I would say "can you have your staff that already inputs report data also upload that data in a standardized way?". Saves your city money, and if it is data that is worth going into workload analysis [it should be standardized and uploaded regularly anyway](https://andrewpwheeler.com/2020/05/13/admin-data-should-be-used-more-often-in-policing-research/) (if it is not worth the time for data entry it is not worth it to include it in the workload analysis).

After the report is done, additionally *you receive the computer code* that I used to conduct the analysis. This is a critical difference in relation to the report by the firm in the Dallas workload study. In this scenario, the Dallas PD paid $500,000 for a 400 page document. With my services, you get the document, but you *also get the computer code* that conducts the analysis. What this means is that if you want to get updated estimates in the future, you can either do them in-house (with your crime analysts), or contract my services again to update the report (for a much smaller fee). ### Raleigh PDs $90,000 Riot Report

In response to George Floyd protests and their subsequent tactical response, the Raleigh PD contracted with a firm to produce an action report and future recommendations [for just under $90,000](https://www.newsobserver.com/news/local/counties/wake-county/article247102782.html). You can [read the final report here](https://s3.documentcloud.org/documents/7327424/RPD-review.pdf), which is a 53 page report with 38 recommendations.

To be clear, I don't necessarily think many of the recommendations are *per se* bad -- but the entire report is almost wholly pure speculation and mostly insipid advice (e.g. "should explore the potential benefits of more rigorously enforcing the ordinances relevant to the permitting of First Amendment protected activity"). Most of it reads like a brochure for a police tech magazine to purchase gizmos.

I have provided services where I provide police agencies an over-view and cost-benefit analysis of technology purchases specifically tailored to your agency. For example, this Raleigh report gives three sentences suggesting the use of CCTV (fixed CCTV is terrible advice for this scenario, mobile units are probably a better overall investment if you want to use them for other scenarios) or helicopter support (reasonable if available, drones are likely a better internal option though).

If your city is interested in CCTV, I can provide an actual report to identify the hot spots of crime (so where the best places to place the cameras are), and calculate estimated crime reductions and clearance rate increases likely to occur in response to those installations. You can then make a local determination if the costs of the technology are worth the investment.

I will not charge you $2000 for a bullet point -- my advice is backed up by a combination of your local data, as well as vetted by the current science on the subject. It will be reasonable and actionable advice, not a wish list of tech purchases that are unlikely to make a large difference in the day to day operations for your police department.
