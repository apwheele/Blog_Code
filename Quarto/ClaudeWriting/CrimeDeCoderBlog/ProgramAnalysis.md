# Program Analysis

What is program analysis? Program analysis (or policy analysis) is the evaluation of a program to see if the program resulted in the intended outcomes. If you have a hotspots policing program, did it cause crime to go down in the hotspots? If you have a gun violence intervention focused on a subset of individuals, did it reduce their involvement in violence? If your county implements bail reforms, did that cause a greater amount of recidivism?

You should conduct a program analysis because even if you implement policies that are shown to be effective in other jurisdictions, they are not guaranteed to work in your jurisdiction. Programs often have substantial costs (even if those costs are simply taking extra time to generate services), so if they are ineffective they are wasting individuals time and money. In the case policies are effective, it may be the case that you want to expand the policy to be more inclusive.

## Evaluating a Policy Already in Place

If possible, it is best to plan a program from the start to be able to effectively identify if it is effective or not. Many real life scenarios prevent this though, especially in the criminal justice and public sector arena. Imagine you are a county interested in [implementing pre-trial risk assessments](https://www.arnoldventures.org/work/release-decision-making) in lieu of bail. It is not feasible to randomize who gets released based on a risk assessment and who receives traditional bail. In that scenario, one needs to create a *quasi-experimental* design to determine whether the risk assessment has increased or decreased recidivism among those pre-trial.

I have extensive experience in conducting quasi-experimental research designs. Example projects in my career include:

 - Evaluating police actions (mostly hot spots oriented) reduced crime in Buffalo (Phillips et al., 2016; ) and Newark (Piza et al., 2020)
 - Evaluating Crime-Prevention-Through-Environmental-Design (CPTED), such as CCTV increasing clearance rates (Jung & Wheeler, 2021), or if installing LED lights decreased violence at nighttime (Wheeler, 2022)
 - Evaluating the effect of noxious properties (and interventions to impact them), such as the spatial spillover effects of bars and medical marijuana dispensaries on crime (Wheeler, 2019; Zakrzewski et al., 2020), home demolitions decreasing crime (Wheeler et al., 2018), or prosecutors targeting enforcement towards problem landlords (Worrall & Wheeler, 2019).

Additionally I am on the editorial board of the [*Journal of Quantitative Criminology*](https://www.springer.com/journal/10940/editors). If you are interested in evaluating a program already in place, I have the experience to generate the most empirically rigorous analysis based on contemporary knowledge of causal inference and research design.

## Planning a Policy from the Start

There are two parts to planning a policy from the start. One, is identifying policies that are worth pursuing. Given my experience in the criminal justice system, I have large knowledge base to draw from in identifying previously successful interventions across the criminal justice system. For example, I have written about violent crime interventions that are successful for the [American Society of Evidence Based Policing](https://americansebp.org/nov_14_2022.php). I additionally regularly peer reviewer for [CrimeSolutions.gov](https://crimesolutions.ojp.gov/). I commonly write on my blog about policies that are not worthwhile as well -- for example, [gun buy backs are likely not effective](https://andrewpwheeler.com/2022/08/21/gun-buy-back-programs-probably-dont-work/), and some gun offender interventions are [too expensive to generate a reasonable return on investment](https://andrewpwheeler.com/2022/05/21/the-limit-on-the-cost-efficiency-of-gun-violence-interventions/).

The second is, once a program has been identified, planning the design in a way to reasonably identify whether the design is effective or not. In terms of evaluating crime reduction policies, I have created different statistical methods to effectively plan research designs, such as how long is necessary to evaluate a program to identify if crime is decreasing.
