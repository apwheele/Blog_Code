## Flock Camera Vulnerabilities Create Evidentiary Problems

Flock Safety cameras are everywhere. Over 5,000 police departments use them. HOAs install them at neighborhood entrances. Shopping centers deploy them in parking lots. The pitch is simple: automated license plate readers that help solve crimes.

Recent security research has revealed [serious vulnerabilities in these devices](https://neuburger.substack.com/p/flock-camera-vulnerability-its-worse). For agencies relying on Flock data as evidence, these flaws raise significant concerns.

### The Vulnerabilities

The security issues fall into two categories: physical access and network-based attacks.

For physical access, researchers found that pressing a button on the device grants root access -- complete administrative control -- with no authentication required. Anyone with physical access to the camera can take it over.

The network vulnerabilities are more concerning for data integrity:

- Cameras can be hijacked to redirect video streams to unauthorized servers
- WiFi credentials can be captured for network infiltration
- Attackers with captured credentials can replace or modify stored footage and images

That last point is the one prosecutors should worry about.

### The Evidentiary Problem

In criminal cases, digital evidence must be authenticated. The prosecution needs to establish that the evidence is what it claims to be and hasn't been tampered with. Chain of custody matters.

If an attacker can modify footage on a Flock camera, defense attorneys have a reasonable basis to challenge the authenticity of any Flock evidence. "How do we know this footage hasn't been altered?" becomes a legitimate question.

This doesn't mean all Flock evidence is inadmissible. But it does mean prosecutors need to be prepared to address these vulnerabilities when introducing Flock data. Depending on the jurisdiction and judge, that could be a significant hurdle.

For civil litigation, the bar is lower, but the same concerns apply. Any party introducing Flock footage should anticipate challenges to its authenticity.

### What This Means for Agencies

Police departments using Flock cameras should:

1. **Audit physical security** of camera installations. If anyone can walk up and press a button, that's a problem.
2. **Document chain of custody** meticulously for any footage used as evidence.
3. **Work with prosecutors** to develop responses to anticipated defense challenges.
4. **Monitor for patches** from Flock addressing these vulnerabilities.

For agencies considering Flock deployments, these vulnerabilities should factor into the procurement decision. Ask Flock directly how they're addressing these issues.

### Beyond License Plates

One thing worth noting: despite being marketed as "license plate readers," these cameras capture everything in their field of view. The vulnerability isn't just about license plate data -- it's about all the video footage these devices collect.

That's a lot of data at risk.
