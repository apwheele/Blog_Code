"For another critique of random patrol, Tim Verlaan, Dan Birks, and Rob van der Mei have a pre-print out that uses agent based simulations to show response time metrics for three different types of police deployments:

 - Random is random patrol within a particular assigned area 
 - static is you take a call, then wait at that same location until dispatched to a new location
 - stationary is you have an optimal location you drive back after your call and wait

They then test these three different deployments in different police districts in Utrecht and with varying number of units to take calls (using actual calls for service and estimated network drive times).

For the majority of scenarios, stationary outperformed static, and static outperformed random.

https://lnkd.in/eXAZHh4X

The work I have done on patrol districting, the optimal location for sufficiently small patrol areas will often be the hotspot in that response district. So it may be that stationary (returning to the hotspot to hang out) is both the optimal in terms of deterring the most crimes *and* being in a location that can get to calls the fastest.

"Most people talk about big data, but analysts are often dealing with small data -- for one application, I developed a statistical test to identify if crimes were randomly distributed according to days of the week with small samples.

The initial event that precipitated was a serial offender stealing catalytic converters from UHauls, it seemed like the events were clustered on certain days of the week, but making the test determined it was consistent with random data. The paper, *Testing Serial Crime Events for Randomness in Day-of-Week Patterns with Small Samples*, has several case studies in addition to the catalytic converter thief (arsons in different areas of the city, and gang shootings). The table I show here are gang shootings, many people default think certain crimes are more likely to occur on weekends, but you can see here they are quite spread out during the week. Again any particular patrol in these high gang shooting areas should not just be done on a single day of the week.

"One of my favorite charts to examine crime patterns is what is called a monthly seasonal chart. The X axis is months, and the Y axis are counts of crimes. Each year gets a new line. 

Often people give each year a different color, but it is much easier to view if you just highlight some years (typically the current year), and then draw the rest as lighter and grey so they are in the background.

This shows the typical ups and downs within a year, as well as whether a year is an outlier. You can see for this chart of robberies in Chicago, they are experiencing monthly lows going back 10 years, and that the yearly peaks are in August-October typically.

This is so low if doing a CompStat meeting, I would actually check the data to make sure there is not a data reporting issue, or if I thought robberies were being displaced to other categories (like car jackings).

"When evaluating the efficacy of interventions to prevent gun violence, one of the factors you need to take into consideration is that gun violence is (thankfully) quite rare per person. The best predictive models for high risk of being involved in a shooting still only have a positive predictive probabilities of around 10%.

https://lnkd.in/guSq8UjK

What this means is that your intervention needs to treat at minimum 10 people to prevent 1 shooting. As most interventions are not going to be 100% effective (the norm in criminology is more like 20-50% for well vetted programs), more realistically you will need to treat 20-50 people *to prevent one shooting*.

I go through cost and benefits example for the READI program in Chicago, which drops $60k per participant (cognitive behavioral therapy + case worker). To me this is impossible ROI from the get go, but if you use some of the value of a statistical life estimates (which in my opinion are highly suspect) you can come up with a positive ROI.

"One of the simple charts I suggest when evaluating predictive crime models is to rank order the predictions, and then on the X axis plot the percent of the sample, and the Y axis the cumulative number of events captured in those top prioritized areas (or people).

https://lnkd.in/e7maCQGV

This has analogies to quite a few different useful pieces of information:

 - can identify if the model can replicate Weisburd's law (5% of area with 50% of crime)
 - the PAI statistic (% crime/ % area), can be inferred, a good predictive model should easily beat Weisburd's law, which is 50%/5% = 10.
 - It is the same as a weighted ROC curve, so if it is a straight line, the model is no better than guessing at random.

Often times higher proportions do not matter, you only care about recall in a fairly small slice, e.g. in the top 100 how much of the crime events I am interested in can I capture.

"A pro-tip when doing data science applications that take along time to run certain inputs is to cache your intermediate results. I often have python functions that look like this:

import os

file_path = ""cached_data.csv.zip""

if os.path.exists(file_path):
  print('loading in data')
  data = pd.read_csv(file_path)
else:
  data = ... function that takes along time to create data ...
  data.to_csv(file_path,index=False)

And for regular pipelines, you can also get the time the cached file was modified via `os.path.getmtime(file_path)`. So if you want you can see how old the cache is, and then redo the calculation if it is too old as well.

Another approach is to use a data based approach to construct areas that are good control areas. One way to do that is via *microsynthetic controls*. "Academics are not a monolith, and so while many are legitimate researchers in criminology and other fields, many are not. For a recent example, there were a set of qualitative criminologists who argued for a theory of ghost criminology -- places that are haunted by their violent histories.

This was absurd, and so mostly ignored (and derided publicly by some) by the folks doing legitimate work. Ian Adam's and company took it one step further, and empirically tested the claims of ghost criminology. You can see how they found a place haunted by its violent past, and show that it has no perceivable effects on participants relative to a control location. You can mildly nudge people to feel uncomfortable, but it is due to direct stimulus, not some paranormal vibes.

https://lnkd.in/erQ3wHQN

It is likely the original group of qualitative criminologists will make excuses and say Ian's work misses the point. Or move on to some other catchy paper title. But this is how real science works -- you put your cards on the table, and then show it with data in the real world.

While ghost criminology is an obvious punching bag, there are other examples of this living in the wild in police science. Fake forensic tools, magical bots to help with mental health issues, gizmos that seem cool. Part of being evidence based is demanding that people show receipts. Just because you claim something works is not sufficient, your clients should be able to demonstrably show it works for specific outcomes.

Otherwise, there is no difference between your cool app and ghosts.

"I often suggest crime analysts to examine hot spots over extended periods of time. Often when analysts are looking at change in hotspots, the tools they use (probably most often just overlaying different KDE maps) tends to be quite noisy.

This blog post I go over the SPPT -- the spatial point pattern test -- and why I like to use that instead of other techniques.

https://lnkd.in/eF-5G3Pj

The SPPT is fairly simple. Imagine in area A, you had 10% of the crime in the prior time period, and in the post time period it had 20% of the crime. The SPPT is just a systematic way to identify if that *percentage* change is statistically significant. 

I have python code linked in the blog post to also take into account multiple comparison corrections. E.g. if you test 100 areas, you would expect some to be statistically different just by chance.

This changes the question to checking for changes in hotspots, to checking for changes in the overall distribution of crime. One of the quarter mile grid cells I show went from 1 theft from MV in the 2019-2023 time period to 19 thefts from MVs in 2024. That area would not be a hotspot (many areas have a larger total volume), but is clearly an area with a noteworthy emerging trend in thefts from MV.

"A useful technique for GIS analysts is *dasymetric mapping*. This is the idea of taking population estimates from one geographic unit, and predicting what it would be from a different set of units.

The white images attached are from my lecture notes on this, and shows the basic idea. Pretend the squares are census blocks, and you want an estimate of the population in the intersecting circle.

These images show the basic idea of proportional areal allocation. But a smarter way for folks in urban areas, you can allocate population to parcels first, and then aggregate back up. NYC has one of the most detailed parcel records, with info like residential feet, that makes it particularly well suited. (On multiple occasions I wish different cities had the number of units in apt complexes in their parcel data.)

Chris Herrmann and colleagues have a paper showing this for NYC, and the last image shows an example of its use. Looking at exposure to particulates from the highway, using the traditional proportional areal allocation immensely over-estimates the actual exposure.

Besides epidemiological studies, it is also useful for emergency planning. E.g. knowing how many people are in an area if that area ever needed evacuation.

Link to Chris's paper, https://lnkd.in/e-Cmbr5g

"One of the common critiques of hotspot policing is that it will just push crime to other places -- what is called displacement. This critique is unfounded though when examining actual data. Many of the hot spot randomized control trials examine this and actually find the opposite -- that there is a diffusion of benefits. Places nearby but outside of the hotspots have additional crime reductions.

Here is a picture from Braga & Weisburd (2022), https://lnkd.in/eQ9wmAvd, showing an average reduction of 7% in the displacement areas across 26 seperate studies.

There are I think a few reasons for this. For why there tends to be not much evidence of displacement -- much crime is opportunistic due to local circumstances in specific areas. Think if you sell drugs at a specific busy bus stop, it is hard to just find another good place to deal with high foot traffic. Not impossible, but difficult, and some people will not take the extra effort.

For the reason for crime displacement, extra hot spot effort in a specific location inevitably results in additional exposure to nearby areas. It is not like you put officers in an isolated box. Hot spots are just intense dosing, and nearby areas get some of that residual dose.

"When crime analysts are making maps of criminal incidents, they almost always use address level geocoding. That is, the point on the map is not exactly where the crime is committed, but is placed at the approximate location of the reported address.

How far away is this approximate location from the actual crime location though? I attempted to answer that, in a paper with Manne Gerrell and Youngmin Yoo, by using more accurate data from Shotspotter compared to the typical address geocoded events.

https://lnkd.in/edfEWv_a

The typical error (95th percentile) was under 350 feet. But there were basically mixtures of types of error. When it is reported at the correct address, the 95th percentile of the error is 260 feet. There are however around 15% of cases in which the address is not even reported as the closest one possible, and in those cases the error is can easily be several hundred feet larger (with wrong streets often around 1000 feet off).

This to me suggests, at least when making hotspot maps or predictive policing models of outdoor crime, it does not make sense to make predictions in any area smaller than 300 feet. (The actual historical crimes may be more often than not in a different grid-cell.)

If you have need for help geocoding data or creating hotspot maps, feel free to get in touch. I have easily geocoded over 1 million crime records in my career across multiple jurisdictions in the US.

"One of the aspects of predictive models that does not seem to be well taught is the idea that you can take aggregate properties of predictions to make decisions. For one example, for pre-trial risk assessments, say you had a cohort of individuals with probabilities of committing future crimes in the next 6 months as:

Person1 - 50%
Person2 - 30%
Person3 - 10%

If you released all three people on their own recognizance, your estimate is that 0.5 + 0.3 + 0.1 = 0.9 crimes will occur in that cohort of three people over the next 6 months. You can use this aggregate information in making determinations for how many people you want detained pre-trial vs how many to release ROR.

https://lnkd.in/eDQURVKJ

Many individuals, especially with binary models, seem to be under the impression that you need to set the threshold for making determinations of ""high risk"" (or here to detain pre-trial) to 50%. Very rarely is that how you should expect to make decisions though.

"Was watching Flock Safety's podcast with Rich Slavin of Scottsdale PD discussing their drone as a first responder (DFR) program. One of the questions Dalton asked was ""how do you measure ROI?"". "One piece of advice for new coders, you need to meticulously *test* your code while you write it. So say I am working with offense data, and then I am changing NIBRS codes to plain text descriptions. In python it will look something like:

```
nibrs_dict = {'200': 'ARSON', .....}
data['nibrs_label'] = data['nibrs_code'].replace(nibrs_dict)
# test to see if the labels are filled in
# data['nibrs_label'].value_counts()
```
Data tends to have many little gotchas, so above if the data for a row was `200 ` (and extra trailing space), the `replace` code would not work as is.

It can seem like a slow process, but even professional software engineers typically only write 100 lines of code a day. Even quite large projects though often will only have a few thousand lines of code.

This is also one area where using LLMs to code is actually harder. When it spits out 500 lines of code, your job turned into verifying those 500 lines (which is a difficult task, and it is not uncommon by the end of the process I have rewritten almost 500 of the lines).

"In ArcGIS online, I had the issue the other day that I wanted to modify a the text length for a field. You unfortunately cannot do this directly (either in the GUI, or using AlterField). But you can do a workaround of creating a temporary new field, copying the data, and then deleting the old field.

So a script like below works:

```
layer = ""https://lnkd.in/e84Jyb8p""
temp_field = ""XXX_temp""
field = ""original_field""
length = 100

# Create a new temp field of the desired length, populate it with old
arcpy.management.AddField(layer,temp_field,""TEXT"",field_length=length)
arcpy.management.CalculateField(layer,temp_field,f""TRIM(TRAILING ' ' FROM {field})"",""SQL"")

# Delete the original field, then remake with the desired length
arcpy.management.DeleteField(layer,field)
```

"When working with `arcpy`, often you need to have tables inside of a geodatabase to use particular geoprocessing tools. Here is an example of taking an external csv file, and importing that file into a geodatabase as a table.

```
import arcpy
gdb = ""./project/LEO_Tables.gdb""
tt = ""TempTable""
arcpy.env.workspace = gdb

# Convert CSV into geodatabase
arcpy.TableToTable_conversion(""YourData.csv"",gdb,tt)
#arcpy.ListTables() # should show that new table

# convert time fields into text, useful for law enforcement management tools
time_fields = ['rep_date','begin','end']
for t in time_fields:
  new_field = f""{t}2""
  arcpy.management.AddField(tt,new_field,""TEXT"")
  arcpy.management.CalculateField(tt,new_field,f""!{t}!.strftime('%Y/%m/%d %H:%m')"", ""PYTHON3"")

# This will show the new fields
#fn = [f.name for f in arcpy.ListFields(tt)]
```

When you create a new project, it automatically creates a geodatabase file to go along with that project. If you just want a standalone geodatabase though, you can use something like this in your python script:

```
import arcpy
import os

gdb = ""./project/LEO_Tables.gdb""

if os.path.exists(gdb):
  pass
else:
  loc, db = os.path.split(gdb)
  arcpy.management.CreateFileGDB(loc,db)
```

So if the geodatabase does not exist, it creates it. If it does exist though, it will not worry about creating a new one.

"I have a new blog post up on Crime De-Coder, some example extensions to the WDD statistic

https://lnkd.in/ef-Esjvv

For those not familiar, I created the WDD stat with Jerry Ratcliffe as a simple way to identify if crime is going down in a treated area relative to control. So say you had:

 - treated area pre=100, post=80
 - control area pre=100, post=90

The point estimate of the crime reduction is -10 (it is -20 in the treated, but the control area is also -10, so the final estimate is -10). I did the math to get a standard error around this estimate (assuming crime follows a Poisson distribution), and you can see the confidence interval of the estimate.

I have shown how to update that simple example for a few different scenarios over the years, so have put them in a blog post + linked to an Excel spreadsheet. Those extensions are:

 - different time periods in pre and post (you gain more precise estimates by using more data)
 - normalizing per area
 - combining multiple estimates together, such as via Harm Weights
 - continuous monitoring over time

"I will have to put in the queue a longer post on kernel density estimates (KDE), but this is a common mistake I see analysts make when using them, so figured would be worth a quick LinkedIn post.

For background on what KDE maps are, imagine for every pin of where crime happened you pour out a bucket of sand. The sand spills out into a mound. For pins of crime nearby each other, the mounds of sand pile up higher. The KDE heatmap visualization is then the height of cumulative sand mounds. For KDE maps, the main parameter of interest is how far out the sand is spread (the bandwidth). The kernel (the shape of the mound) does not matter all that much.

When you plot a map with only a few crimes, you get these little hotspots, but all it is is a single crime. The combo of a bad default bandwidth (default for crime analysis should often be something like 500-1000 feet), and the bad legend combine to make mountains out of mole hills. 

When plotting over time, this becomes especially problematic, as you are saying hotspots shift with the coming and going of singular crimes. It is mostly just noise though, one more crime in the area does not establish a significant change in the historical pattern.

The solution? IMO your products should just use the points for short time windows. For even long ones just having a table of repeat problem addresses is likely better than the ever shifting KDE maps.

This example is via Craig Uchida's group Justice Security Strategies. Who do good work but I don't think this example is the best way to approach the problem.

"Big data is not as much a buzzword now as it used to be, but it was always a bit over-hyped. Most laptops anymore have at least 8 gigs of RAM, and with that you can probably hold a 1 million row table in memory (plus have a Chrome tab opened).

If you do happen to have a table that is too big to fit into memory, one solution is to read the table in smaller chunks. So in python, reading a table from a SQL query would look something like:

 ```
conn = ...sql connection...
for ch in pd.read_sql('SELECT * FROM table',conn,chunksize=20000):
    # ch is a pandas dataframe
    # do whatever operation you want on the smaller chunk of 20k
    # rows, and iterate over the values
```

And so even if `SELECT * FROM table` is too large to fit into memory all at once, the smaller chunks should be fine to work with.

The inner loop for example, I have pipelines that apply predictive models to the rows of the table and write back to another database table. Doing it this way I know the process will never run out of RAM.

This blog post shows an example of memory profiling with different databases in python for similar chunked queries.

"As a crime analyst, I hated percent change. Crime is up 100% this month! Well, it went from 5 last month to 10 this month, is that an actual change we should worry about?

Imagine you have crime data that averages 100 events per year. If you had a year with 101 would you say that is a substantive increase? How about 110? 120? 150? Where exactly is the difference between normal year to year variation vs we think that something fundamentally changed in the underlying process?

So I suggest analysts use what I call the Poisson Z-score. It is a super simple formula:

  2*[sqrt(new) - sqrt(old)] = Z

And if the crime data is close to Poisson distributed (no real life process exactly follows a hypothetical stat distribution, but is often close enough in practice). When doing the square root transformation, this metric is approximately a standard normal distribution.

The image in this post is a simulation I conducted -- I generated random Poisson data with a particular mean, then calculated Percent change in the top panel, and Poisson Z in the bottom. In the top panel, even for crime data with means of 20 and 50, you can get random data have a 100% increase (percent change is asymmetric, going from 10 to 20 is a 100% increase, but going from 20 to 10 is a 50% decrease, it is biased to say bigger increases).

In the bottom panel, you can see the data is more evenly distributed. Because real life crime data is often slightly more dispersed than Poisson data, and analysts typically monitor many crime series, I suggest analysts alarm at +/-3 or +/-4, and not the typical +/-2 that academics more often use.

What do analysts do then when a series alarms? That is just the start, it is just a signal it may be worth your time to investigate the data further to see if you can uncover some aspect that is causing the spike up. Or the obverse, if something went down in response to something the PD did, that is information people would like to know as well!

"From what I have seen, Peregrine has a very nice data integration product. That said, having real time data is not a significant impediment to implementing CompStat -- you can go to CompStat meetings with week old data and be fine.

The generic recipe for CompStat is:

 A) identify problem
 B) take actions to solve problem
 C) later see if action was actually taken
 D) see if action solved the problem
 E) if solved, find another problem, if not, go back to (B) with a new action

I do quite a bit of number crunching, but honestly A is not that hard. B is hard, in that many people will say ""I am going to do this thing"" and do not actually do that thing. Hence I make step C, check to see if person did that thing.

Doing that thing is not guaranteed to solve the problem. All you can do is make sure people are following the process they set out to do. Most people do not, there needs to be very strong follow up/top down pressure to make sure people actually do things. (This tends to look more like effective project management than crime analysis in practice.)

Then part D, seeing if the problem is solved. This takes more rigorous data, but again does not need to be real time. We are talking big problems, showing there were no incidents yesterday does not show the problem is solved. You will need to look at bigger time windows.

Peregrine I do not doubt is a great product for real time investigations. It will not intrinsically improve any CompStat process. Those steps C & D above are to me the parts where most agencies have the hardest time.

"If you are doing public opinion surveys (or having a firm do them for you), the analysis should do what is called post-stratification of the survey results.

Traditionally surveys were designed with weights, with complicated surveys often having design based weights for oversampling populations of interest. In practice, because of survey non-response (which has gotten much worse over the past several decades), weights are not an accurate way to estimate surveys.

Probably just as common though are convenience samples for surveys in criminal justice. Just put a survey online for example. In these scenarios, you don't even have weights. But if you collect the demographics of the survey respondents (age, race, gender), you can likely use post-stratification to get a very reasonable estimate of public opinion.

The idea is to fit a regression equation predicting the outcome, to keep it simple I will pretend we just have two groups, A & B. Pretend our regression equation predicting the outcome is `Opinion = 1.2*A + 0.8*B`. Now for our estimate of opinion in an area, say we had 300 in group A, and 100 in group B. Our area estimate will then be `(1.2*300 + 0.8*100)/400 = 1.1`. You then can incorporate errors into the estimates via the regression equation errors.

In many convenience samples, this will adjust the self selected sample (which are often much older individuals), and make the overall estimates much more representative of your population. "Most academic work on fraud detection is not practical -- papers with complicated models that no one can implement. This paper by Richard Frank and Ashleigh Burnside is different.

https://www.crimrxiv.com/pub/k82mf4xe/release/1

They took 37 FINTRAC suspicious transaction reports (3,263 transactions across casinos, banks, and businesses) and used two lightweight techniques: Benford's Law and feature engineering for anomaly detection.

Benford's Law is simple -- in naturally occurring data, the first digit should be 1 about 30% of the time, 2 about 18% of the time, etc. For second digits, you expect 0 to appear about 12% of the time. In their casino/banking data, second digit 0 showed up 52.9% of the time. That is a massive red flag.

They also engineered features like "gaming versatility" -- how many distinct casinos a person or business uses. More venues = more likely they are structuring transactions to avoid detection. Bank drafts were another indicator, as they are a common tool in the Vancouver Model of money laundering.

The result? They reduced 671 entities down to 58 high-priority targets using Excel, Python, and Tableau. No enterprise software, no complicated machine learning. Just smart feature engineering and visual triage.

This is how fraud detection should be done -- tools that work in resource-constrained environments and that analysts can actually understand and implement.

I generally try to be positive in my posts, but this clip by Flock CEO Garrett Langley saying that Flock's tools *mainly* operate via deterrence (making offenders think twice before committing a crime) and subsequently reduce incarceration is beyond the pale.

To start, Flock ALPRs no doubt help solve crimes. They do this via hot listing stolen vehicles, so officers get in the moment alerts where a stolen is located at. They also can help monitor movements of individuals to/from crime scenes when linked with a vehicle. Cameras, by their nature, cannot physically prevent a crime.

The fact that most offenders are impulsive is definitely true -- it is a long standing criminological finding. (There is a whole branch of criminology that this fact is a major keystone -- self-control theory.) This means many criminals are not deterred, even if a camera is staring them in the face! They tend to not be forward thinking.

The criminology literature on the efficacy of cameras is actually quite mixed (and difficult to identify the difference between specific incapacitation vs deterrence). One of the findings Eric Piza continually points to is the cameras often are not effective on their own -- the idea you can just put up a bunch of cameras and folks magically stop committing crime is not true.

I get it is a nice message for the anti-policing crowd -- and I agree prisons are expensive. You have a small number of people that continually commit crimes over and over again (no point in arguing whether these folks are evil or not, they are certainly impulsive though). Helping catch those people is the main draw of Flock.
